Welcome!

Welcome to the ultimate fanpage for Baldur's Gate! A place to dive in and explore the main lore, hidden lore, characters, and my own personal opinions about each one of the characters and the story. As a big fan of Larian Studios and this game, this page is basically a love letter to this masterpiece. There is no other way to describe this game than by calling it a masterpiece. There's the already established Dungeons & Dragons Lore to further enrich the story of the game, mechanics, and gameplay that are second to none, and amazingly well-written characters, dialogues, and secrets. Truly, this game is a work of art.

Baldur's Gate 3 is a role-playing video game that is in the universe of Dungeons & Dragons, an already well-established and ever-growing fantasy universe. And this is not only a game that has races and mechanics from the D&D universe, but it is also a game that has characters from the D&D universe, such as Minsc and Boo, who are fan-favorites. Some locations appear in D&D lore, such as Baldur's Gate, the Underdark, Avernus, the Whispering Depths, and many more. The game is set in the Forgotten Realms, one of the most popular settings in Dungeons & Dragons.



Main Story

Baldur’s Gate 3 starts with one of the strongest hooks I’ve ever seen in an RPG: you wake up abducted by mind flayers, a parasite squirming in your brain, and the promise that you should already be dead. From the very beginning, the game makes you live with that ticking clock, pushing you to search for a cure while constantly tempting you with the strange power the tadpole offers. What really elevates it is how personal everything feels—your companions aren’t just party members, they’re people with their own fears, ambitions, and secrets, all wrestling with the same looming transformation. The early journey through the wilderness, Emerald Grove, and the Goblin Camp sets the tone perfectly: there are no clean victories, only choices that echo later. Even when you do the “right” thing, someone always pays the price.

As the story moves into the Shadow-Cursed Lands, the mood shifts hard, and I love it for that. Hope becomes fragile, rare, and fiercely defended, especially at places like Last Light Inn. The narrative tightens around the Absolute, revealing that this isn’t just another cult, but a carefully constructed machine of control tied directly to mind flayers and stolen divine power. Moonrise Towers is where the mask fully comes off, and the game stops pretending this is a simple good-versus-evil tale. You start realizing how deep the manipulation goes, how many lives are being used as fuel, and how close your own fate is to becoming part of that system.

By the time you reach Baldur’s Gate itself, the story feels massive in scale but still incredibly intimate. Political intrigue, ancient legends, personal vendettas, and godly schemes all collide as the truth behind the Absolute fully unravels. The city doesn’t feel like a reward at the end of the road—it feels like a pressure cooker ready to explode. Your earlier choices come back in ways that feel earned, sometimes painfully so, and the companions’ arcs reach moments that are genuinely emotional, whether tragic, redemptive, or bittersweet. The villains aren’t just obstacles anymore; they’re reflections of what happens when power is taken without restraint.

The final act brings everything together in a way that feels bold and unapologetically D&D. Gods interfere, ancient powers awaken, and the question of free will versus control becomes unavoidable. The game forces you to confront what you’ve been doing with the tadpole’s power all along and whether the ends ever justified the means. What really stuck with me is that there’s no single “perfect” ending—only the one you earn through your decisions. Baldur’s Gate 3 isn’t just about saving the world; it’s about who you become while trying, and that’s why, as a fan, it hits so hard and stays with you long after the credits roll.
